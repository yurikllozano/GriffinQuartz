<p>If you live in South Florida, your home faces conditions that most of the country never experiences. Relentless humidity, salt-laden air, intense UV exposure, and the threat of hurricanes create an environment that demands more from every surface in your home &mdash; especially your countertops.</p>

<p>Whether you are building new construction in <a href="/locations/boca-raton">Boca Raton</a>, renovating a waterfront condo in <a href="/locations/fort-lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a>, or upgrading your <a href="/locations/miami">Miami</a> kitchen, the material you choose needs to perform under conditions that would damage lesser surfaces. This guide covers everything South Florida homeowners need to know about selecting, installing, and maintaining countertops that thrive in our unique climate.</p>

<p>At Griffin Quartz, we have fabricated and installed thousands of countertops across Palm Beach County, Broward County, and Miami-Dade County. That firsthand experience informs every recommendation in this guide. We know what works here because we live and work here.</p>

<h2>Climate Factors That Affect Countertops in South Florida</h2>

<p>Before you choose a countertop material, it helps to understand exactly what South Florida's climate will throw at it. These environmental factors should be the starting point for any material decision.</p>

<h3>Humidity and Moisture</h3>

<p>South Florida's relative humidity regularly hovers between 70% and 90%, with summer months often pushing higher. This constant moisture affects countertop materials in several ways. Porous surfaces like unsealed marble or concrete can absorb moisture, leading to staining, warping, or mold growth over time. Even the adhesives and sealants used during installation must be rated for high-humidity environments.</p>

<p>Non-porous materials like quartz have a distinct advantage in South Florida because they do not absorb moisture at any point on the surface. This means no sealing, no mold concerns, and no moisture-related damage &mdash; even in a bathroom vanity next to a steamy shower or a kitchen near an open lanai.</p>

<h3>Salt Air Exposure</h3>

<p>Properties within a few miles of the Atlantic Ocean or the Intracoastal Waterway are constantly exposed to salt-laden air. This corrosive environment accelerates wear on many building materials. Metal fixtures tarnish faster, wood deteriorates more quickly, and certain stone surfaces can develop pitting or surface degradation.</p>

<p>For waterfront homes in <a href="/locations/delray-beach">Delray Beach</a>, <a href="/locations/pompano-beach">Pompano Beach</a>, or <a href="/locations/deerfield-beach">Deerfield Beach</a>, choosing a countertop material that resists salt corrosion is not optional &mdash; it is essential. Quartz and granite both perform well in salt air environments, though quartz requires less ongoing maintenance to keep performing at its best.</p>

<h3>UV Exposure and Sunlight</h3>

<p>South Florida receives some of the most intense UV radiation in the continental United States. Our homes are designed to let in natural light, with large windows, sliding glass doors, and open floor plans that blur the line between indoor and outdoor living. That abundant sunlight looks beautiful, but it can cause certain countertop materials to discolor over time.</p>

<p>This is particularly relevant for countertops near windows, in sunrooms, or in open-concept kitchens that face south or west. Standard quartz can be affected by prolonged direct UV exposure, which is one reason we always discuss window orientation and light exposure during our design consultations. For areas with heavy sun exposure, UV-resistant quartz formulations or natural granite may be the better choice.</p>

<h3>Hurricane Preparedness</h3>

<p>Hurricane season runs from June through November, and South Florida homeowners know the drill. While countertops themselves rarely sustain direct hurricane damage (they are heavy and well-secured), the surrounding conditions matter. Flying debris can shatter windows, allowing rain and wind into kitchens. Power outages lead to spoiled food sitting on counters for extended periods. Flooding can affect lower-level installations.</p>

<p>Non-porous countertop materials recover better from these events. Quartz can be thoroughly cleaned and disinfected after storm exposure without any lasting damage. Porous materials like marble or unsealed granite may absorb contaminants that are difficult to fully remove.</p>

<h2>Best Countertop Materials for South Florida's Climate</h2>

<p>Not every countertop material performs equally in South Florida's demanding environment. Here is an honest assessment of the most popular options and how they hold up in our climate.</p>

<h3>Quartz: The Top Choice for Indoor Spaces</h3>

<p>Engineered quartz consistently ranks as the best countertop material for South Florida indoor spaces. Its non-porous surface makes it naturally resistant to humidity, moisture, stains, and bacterial growth. It never needs sealing, handles daily use without showing wear, and comes in hundreds of colors and patterns that suit every design aesthetic.</p>

<p><strong>Why quartz excels in South Florida:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li>Zero porosity means humidity and moisture have no effect</li>
    <li>Resists mold and mildew growth in damp environments</li>
    <li>Salt air cannot penetrate or degrade the surface</li>
    <li>Consistent color and pattern from slab to slab</li>
    <li>Maintenance-free: just soap and water</li>
    <li>Available in coastal-inspired colors and patterns</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>One consideration:</strong> Standard quartz is not recommended for direct, prolonged UV exposure. For covered outdoor kitchens and indoor spaces, it is the ideal choice. For fully exposed outdoor applications, consider the alternatives below.</p>

<h3>Granite: Best for Outdoor and Sun-Exposed Areas</h3>

<p>Natural granite is formed under extreme heat and pressure deep within the earth, giving it inherent resistance to UV radiation and temperature extremes. This makes granite the top choice for fully exposed outdoor kitchens, pool areas, and countertops that receive direct sunlight for extended periods.</p>

<p><strong>Granite's strengths in South Florida:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li>Natural UV resistance prevents fading and discoloration</li>
    <li>Handles extreme heat from grills and direct sun</li>
    <li>Withstands tropical storm conditions</li>
    <li>Unique natural patterns add character to outdoor spaces</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Maintenance note:</strong> Granite is porous and requires sealing once or twice per year, especially in South Florida's humid climate. Unsealed granite can absorb moisture, oils, and stains. With proper maintenance, granite countertops last decades.</p>

<h3>Quartzite: The Premium Natural Stone Option</h3>

<p>Quartzite is a natural stone that combines the beauty of marble with durability that exceeds granite. It resists heat, UV exposure, and scratching, making it an excellent premium option for South Florida homeowners who want a natural stone look with superior performance.</p>

<p>Quartzite does require periodic sealing, similar to granite, but its density and hardness make it one of the most durable natural stones available. Popular quartzite options like Taj Mahal, Sea Pearl, and Calacatta quartzite offer stunning aesthetics that complement coastal architecture.</p>

<h3>Marble: Beautiful but High-Maintenance</h3>

<p>Marble is undeniably gorgeous, and it has a long history in South Florida luxury homes. However, it is the most demanding material in our climate. Marble is porous, soft, and prone to etching from acidic substances. In a humid environment, it requires diligent sealing and careful maintenance to stay looking its best.</p>

<p>If you love the look of marble, we often recommend Calacatta-inspired quartz as an alternative. You get the dramatic veining and elegant appearance of marble with the zero-maintenance performance of quartz. It is one of the most popular choices among our South Florida clients.</p>

<h2>Outdoor Kitchen Countertops in South Florida</h2>

<p>South Florida's outdoor kitchen market is booming. Year-round warm weather, a culture built around pool parties and waterfront entertaining, and properties with generous outdoor living spaces make outdoor kitchens a smart investment that adds both enjoyment and resale value to your home.</p>

<p>Choosing countertops for an outdoor kitchen requires different thinking than indoor applications. Exposure to rain, sun, salt air, and temperature fluctuations adds stress that indoor countertops never face. The level of exposure your outdoor kitchen has determines which materials will serve you best.</p>

<h3>Covered Outdoor Kitchens</h3>

<p>If your outdoor kitchen is under a solid roof, pergola, lanai, or screened enclosure, quartz is an excellent choice. The covering protects against direct UV exposure (the one condition quartz does not handle well), while quartz's non-porous surface handles humidity, rain spray, spills, and heavy use without issue.</p>

<h3>Fully Exposed Outdoor Kitchens</h3>

<p>For outdoor cooking areas that receive direct sunlight, natural granite is the gold standard. Its inherent UV resistance means it will not fade or discolor, and its heat tolerance handles everything from a 95-degree August afternoon to a searing hot grill grate.</p>

<h2>Coastal Design Trends: Light, Bright, and Natural</h2>

<p>South Florida's design aesthetic is distinct from the rest of the country. Our homes draw inspiration from the ocean, tropical landscapes, and an indoor-outdoor lifestyle that makes the most of our year-round sunshine. These influences shape the countertop colors, patterns, and styles that dominate the region.</p>

<h3>Coastal Modern</h3>

<p>The most popular kitchen design style across South Florida right now blends clean modern lines with warm coastal elements. Think white or light gray quartz countertops paired with natural wood cabinets, matte brass or brushed nickel hardware, and plenty of natural light. Calacatta-inspired quartz with soft gray veining is the single most requested pattern in our showroom.</p>

<h3>Tropical Contemporary</h3>

<p>This style embraces South Florida's tropical setting with bolder choices. Rich greens, warm woods, and natural stone textures create kitchens that feel like luxury resorts. Quartzite with dramatic movement, book-matched marble-look quartz, and earthy granite tones all work beautifully in this style.</p>

<h3>Mediterranean Revival</h3>

<p>Boca Raton, Coral Gables, and parts of Palm Beach have a strong Mediterranean architectural tradition. Countertops in these homes tend toward warm creams, golden tones, and natural stone with visible character. Honed finishes and leathered granite add texture and old-world charm.</p>

<h2>Condo Renovations: Special Considerations for South Florida</h2>

<p>A significant portion of South Florida's housing stock consists of condominiums and high-rise residences. Renovating a condo kitchen is fundamentally different from renovating a single-family home, and these differences directly affect your countertop project.</p>

<h3>HOA Rules and Approval Processes</h3>

<p>Nearly every South Florida condo association requires formal approval before any renovation can begin. This typically involves submitting an alteration application, providing contractor insurance certificates (COIs), detailing the materials and scope of work, and sometimes presenting before the board. Approval timelines range from two weeks to two months depending on the building.</p>

<p>At Griffin Quartz, we handle all HOA documentation for our condo clients. We carry the insurance coverage that buildings require and are familiar with the approval processes at dozens of South Florida condominium complexes.</p>

<h3>Elevator Access and Logistics</h3>

<p>Getting full-size countertop slabs into a high-rise unit presents unique logistical challenges. Freight elevator dimensions, weight limits, and scheduling restrictions all need to be coordinated in advance. Some buildings only allow construction deliveries during specific hours, and some require floor protection in hallways and elevators.</p>

<p>Our installation teams have completed projects in buildings across <a href="/locations/fort-lauderdale">Fort Lauderdale</a>, <a href="/locations/boca-raton">Boca Raton</a>, <a href="/locations/miami">Miami</a>, and <a href="/locations/west-palm-beach">West Palm Beach</a>. We know the procedures and we coordinate directly with building management so you do not have to.</p>

<h3>Noise and Construction Hours</h3>

<p>Most South Florida condos restrict construction noise to weekday business hours, typically 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM. Some buildings have additional blackout periods around holidays or seasonal resident populations. Countertop installation itself is relatively quiet compared to full kitchen renovations, but templating, demolition of old counters, and cutting adjustments do produce noise that neighbors will hear.</p>

<h2>Popular Countertop Colors in South Florida</h2>

<p>Color trends in South Florida kitchens differ from national trends because our light, climate, and architecture are different. The abundant natural light that pours through large windows and sliding glass doors means colors read differently here than they would in a dimmer northern kitchen.</p>

<h3>Whites and Creams</h3>

<p>White countertops dominate South Florida kitchens, and for good reason. They reflect our abundant natural light, create an airy feeling that complements open floor plans, and pair perfectly with the ocean views and tropical landscapes visible through windows. Calacatta-inspired quartz (white with bold gray veining) and Carrara-look quartz (white with softer, more dispersed veining) are the two most popular styles.</p>

<h3>Warm Grays</h3>

<p>Gray countertops offer a sophisticated alternative to white while still keeping kitchens bright and contemporary. Warm grays with subtle veining work particularly well with white or light wood cabinetry. This palette has become especially popular in new construction across <a href="/locations/boca-raton">Boca Raton</a> and <a href="/locations/delray-beach">Delray Beach</a>.</p>

<h3>Warm Neutrals and Taupes</h3>

<p>As a counterpoint to the cool whites and grays that have dominated for the past decade, warm neutrals are gaining traction in South Florida. Creamy taupes, soft beiges, and warm whites create kitchens that feel inviting and relaxed &mdash; perfectly suited to the Florida lifestyle.</p>

<h3>Bold Veining and Statement Stones</h3>

<p>For homeowners who want their countertop to be the focal point of the kitchen, dramatic veining is the way to go. Book-matched quartz slabs with large-scale veining, dark quartz with gold veining, and natural quartzite with bold movement all make stunning statement pieces, especially on large kitchen islands.</p>

<h2>What Is a Waterfall Edge Countertop?</h2>

<p>A waterfall edge countertop is a design where the countertop material continues vertically down one or both sides of a kitchen island or peninsula, flowing from the horizontal surface to the floor like a waterfall. Instead of ending at the edge of the cabinetry with a standard overhang, the stone cascades to the ground, creating a seamless, dramatic visual effect.</p>

<p>This design element has become one of the most requested features in modern kitchen design. It transforms a kitchen island from a functional work surface into a striking architectural statement. The waterfall edge works particularly well with veined materials like Calacatta quartz, marble, and quartzite, where the pattern can be matched across the horizontal and vertical surfaces for a continuous flow of veining.</p>

<p>Waterfall edges are not limited to kitchen islands. You will also see them on bathroom vanities, reception desks, conference tables, and bar tops. However, the kitchen island remains the most common and impactful application. For a comprehensive look at kitchen countertop options and layouts, our <a href="/blog/kitchen-countertops-complete-guide">complete kitchen countertops guide</a> covers everything from material selection to island configurations.</p>

<h2>Single Waterfall vs. Double Waterfall</h2>

<p>When planning a waterfall edge island, one of the first decisions is whether to do a single waterfall (one side) or a double waterfall (both sides).</p>

<h3>Single Waterfall</h3>

<p>A single waterfall features the stone cascading down just one end of the island. This is the more common and budget-friendly option. Most homeowners choose to place the waterfall on the end that is most visible from the main living area, creating a focal point without doubling the material cost.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Islands against a wall on one side, kitchens where one end of the island faces the living room, and budget-conscious projects that still want the dramatic waterfall look.</p>

<h3>Double Waterfall</h3>

<p>A double waterfall features the stone flowing down both ends of the island. This creates a fully enclosed, monolithic look that is particularly striking with veined materials. The island appears to be carved from a single block of stone, which adds a sculptural quality to the kitchen.</p>

<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Freestanding islands visible from all sides, open-concept floor plans, and high-end kitchens where the island is the centerpiece of the design.</p>

<h3>Cost Difference</h3>

<p>A double waterfall typically costs 60% to 80% more than a single waterfall in additional material and fabrication, not double the price. The fabrication process for the second panel is simpler since the measurements and techniques are already established from the first side.</p>

<h2>Best Materials for Waterfall Edges</h2>

<p>Not every countertop material is equally suited for a waterfall edge. The visual impact of the design depends heavily on how the material looks when the pattern transitions from horizontal to vertical.</p>

<h3>Quartz</h3>

<p>Quartz is the most popular material for waterfall edges, and for good reason. Engineered quartz offers consistent patterns that can be precisely matched at the seam where the horizontal surface meets the vertical panel. Many quartz manufacturers now produce slabs specifically designed with waterfall applications in mind, featuring veining patterns that look stunning when they cascade down the side of an island.</p>

<p><strong>Advantages for waterfall edges:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li>Consistent patterning makes seam matching more predictable</li>
    <li>Non-porous surface means the vertical panel is easy to clean</li>
    <li>Wide range of veined patterns that enhance the waterfall effect</li>
    <li>More affordable than natural stone alternatives</li>
    <li>Durable enough to withstand bumps from chairs and foot traffic</li>

<h3>Marble</h3>

<p>Natural marble creates arguably the most breathtaking waterfall edges. <h3>Quartzite</h3>

<p>Quartzite offers the best of both worlds: natural stone beauty with hardness that exceeds granite. Materials like Taj Mahal quartzite and Calacatta quartzite produce stunning waterfall edges with dramatic natural movement. Quartzite is more expensive than quartz but requires less maintenance than marble.</p>

<h2>How the Seam Works: Mitered Edges and Book-Matching</h2>

<p>The quality of a waterfall edge depends almost entirely on how the seam at the corner is fabricated. This is where skilled craftsmanship separates an impressive waterfall from a disappointing one.</p>

<h3>Mitered Seams</h3>

<p>The standard technique for waterfall edges is a mitered seam, where both the horizontal countertop and the vertical panel are cut at a 45-degree angle at the corner. When these two angled pieces are joined, they create a clean 90-degree corner with a nearly invisible seam line. The miter is reinforced with adhesive and mechanical fasteners hidden inside the joint.</p>

<p>A well-executed mitered seam should be barely visible &mdash; you should see a continuous flow of material from the top surface down the side. This requires precision cutting, careful alignment, and an experienced fabrication team. For details on edge profiles and fabrication techniques, read our <a href="/blog/quartz-countertop-edge-profiles">guide to edge profiles</a>.</p>

<h3>Book-Matching</h3>

<p>Book-matching is the technique of cutting consecutive slabs (or sections of the same slab) and opening them like the pages of a book, creating a mirror image of the pattern on either side of the seam. When applied to a waterfall edge, book-matching means the veining on the horizontal surface mirrors and continues into the veining on the vertical panel.</p>

<p>This technique produces the most visually stunning waterfall edges, particularly with heavily veined materials. It does require more material (you need adjacent sections of the same slab) and more fabrication time, which adds to the cost. Not all fabricators offer book-matching, so this is an important question to ask when getting quotes.</p>

<h2>Can You Use Quartz Outdoors? The Honest Answer</h2>

<p>We love quartz countertops. We install them in hundreds of kitchens every year, and there is no material we recommend more confidently for indoor applications. But honesty is more important than making a sale, so here is the straight answer.</p>

<p><strong>Quartz is not the ideal material for outdoor countertops that are exposed to direct sunlight.</strong></p>

<p>Here is why. Quartz countertops are engineered from approximately 90-93% natural quartz crystals bound together with 7-10% polymer resins. Those resins are what make quartz non-porous, stain-resistant, and maintenance-free. But those same resins are vulnerable to prolonged ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun.</p>

<h3>What Happens to Quartz in Direct Sunlight</h3>

<p>When quartz countertops are exposed to direct UV radiation over extended periods, two things can happen:</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>Discoloration:</strong> The resin binders can yellow or shift color, particularly noticeable in white and light-colored quartz. A countertop that started as a bright white may develop a yellowish or amber tone over months of sun exposure.</li>
    <li><strong>Warping or cracking:</strong> In extreme cases, prolonged heat and UV exposure can cause the resin to expand unevenly, potentially leading to warping, hazing, or surface cracking. South Florida's intense sun makes this risk higher than in milder climates.</li>
</ul>

<p>Most quartz manufacturers explicitly state in their warranties that UV damage from outdoor use is not covered. This is not a technicality &mdash; it is because they know the material has limitations in direct sun exposure.</p>

<h3>When Quartz Can Work Outdoors</h3>

<p>That said, quartz can perform well in <strong>covered outdoor spaces</strong> where it is protected from direct sunlight. If your outdoor kitchen is under a solid roof, inside a screened lanai, under a dense pergola, or in a fully shaded area that never receives direct sun, quartz can be a viable option. In these settings, quartz delivers the same maintenance-free performance it provides indoors.</p>

<p><strong>Conditions where outdoor quartz can work:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li>Under a solid roof or fully covered patio</li>
    <li>Inside a screened lanai or enclosed porch</li>
    <li>Under a pergola with sufficient shade coverage</li>
    <li>In an outdoor kitchen area that faces north and receives minimal direct sun</li>

<p><strong>Conditions where quartz should NOT be used:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li>Open-air outdoor kitchens without overhead coverage</li>
    <li>Pool deck cooking areas with direct sun exposure</li>
    <li>South- or west-facing outdoor spaces with afternoon sun</li>
    <li>Any area where the countertop receives more than 2-3 hours of direct sunlight per day</li>
</ul>

<h2>Best Alternatives for Outdoor Kitchen Countertops</h2>

<p>If your outdoor kitchen is fully or partially exposed to direct sun, these materials will serve you better than quartz. Each has its own strengths, and the right choice depends on your design preferences, budget, and willingness to maintain the surface.</p>

<h3>Granite</h3>

<p>Natural granite is the gold standard for fully exposed outdoor kitchens. As an igneous rock formed under extreme heat and pressure, granite has inherent resistance to UV radiation, heat, and weather. It will not fade, warp, or degrade in direct sunlight, making it the most reliable choice for South Florida outdoor cooking spaces.</p>

<p><strong>Pros:</strong> UV resistant, extremely heat tolerant, beautiful natural patterns, proven outdoor durability.</p>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong> Requires annual sealing, can stain if unsealed, limited pattern consistency between slabs.</p>

<h3>Quartzite</h3>

<p>Natural quartzite (not to be confused with engineered quartz) is a metamorphic rock that combines stunning natural beauty with exceptional hardness. It resists UV exposure, heat, and scratching, making it an excellent premium outdoor option. For more on how these two materials compare, see our <a href="/blog/countertop-material-comparison-guide">countertop material comparison guide</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Pros:</strong> UV resistant, extremely hard, gorgeous natural veining, heat tolerant.</p>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong> Expensive, requires sealing, limited color options compared to engineered materials.</p>

<h3>Concrete</h3>

<p>Concrete countertops offer maximum customization for outdoor kitchens. They can be poured in any shape, colored to match any design scheme, and cast on-site for a seamless fit. Properly sealed concrete handles outdoor conditions well, though it requires more maintenance than stone.</p>

<p><strong>Pros:</strong> Fully customizable, can be cast in any shape, heat resistant, develops unique patina.</p>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong> Requires sealing every 1-3 years, can crack over time, heavier than stone alternatives.</p>

<h3>Porcelain Slabs</h3>

<p>Large-format porcelain slabs are a newer option gaining popularity for outdoor kitchens. UV resistant, non-porous, scratch resistant, and available in realistic stone-look patterns, porcelain offers many of the benefits of quartz without the UV vulnerability.</p>

<p><strong>Pros:</strong> UV resistant, non-porous, lightweight, low maintenance, realistic stone patterns.</p>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong> Can chip on edges, limited thickness options, fewer fabricators experienced with the material.</p>

<h3>Natural Stone Tile</h3>

<p>For budget-conscious outdoor kitchen projects, natural stone tile (travertine, slate, or flagstone) provides a weather-resistant surface at a lower price point than full slab materials. The grout lines require maintenance, but the stone itself handles outdoor conditions well.</p>

<p><strong>Pros:</strong> Affordable, UV resistant, wide variety of styles, easy to repair individual tiles.</p>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong> Grout requires maintenance, not as seamless as slab materials, can feel uneven.</p>

<h2>Maintenance in the South Florida Climate</h2>

<p>Outdoor countertops in South Florida face harsher conditions than indoor surfaces, and proper maintenance extends their lifespan and keeps them looking their best.</p>

<h3>Regular Cleaning</h3>

<p>Clean outdoor countertops at least weekly, even when not in use. South Florida's humidity, tree pollen, and airborne salt create a film on surfaces that can stain or degrade certain materials over time. Warm water and mild dish soap is sufficient for routine cleaning on any material.</p>

<h3>Sealing Schedule</h3>

<p>Natural stone (granite and quartzite) should be sealed once or twice per year for outdoor applications. Concrete requires sealing every one to three years. A simple water test can tell you when it is time to reseal: sprinkle a few drops of water on the surface. If the water beads up, the seal is intact. If it soaks in and darkens the stone, it is time to reseal.</p>

<h3>Hurricane and Storm Preparation</h3>

<p>Before a major storm, cover outdoor countertops with moving blankets or plywood to protect against flying debris. After the storm, inspect for chips or cracks, clear debris carefully (do not drag heavy objects across the surface), and clean thoroughly to remove any salt or contaminants deposited by the storm.</p>

<h3>Salt Air Maintenance</h3>

<p>Waterfront properties should rinse outdoor countertops with fresh water weekly to prevent salt accumulation. Salt deposits left on natural stone can penetrate the surface and cause pitting or staining, even when the stone is sealed. A quick rinse with a garden hose takes seconds and prevents long-term damage.</p>
