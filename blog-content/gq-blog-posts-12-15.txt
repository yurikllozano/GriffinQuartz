<h2>How to Choose the Right Cambria Color</h2>

<p>With over 200 options, narrowing down your choice requires a systematic approach. Here is how we guide homeowners through the selection process at our Boca Raton showroom.</p>

<h3>Step 1: Start With Your Cabinetry</h3>

<p>Your countertop will be viewed against your cabinets more than any other element. White cabinets give you the most flexibility. Dark cabinets call for lighter countertops to provide contrast. Wood cabinets work best with warm-toned Cambria colors like Swanbridge or Brittanicca Warm.</p>

<h3>Step 2: Consider Your Lighting</h3>

<p>South Florida homes are blessed with abundant natural light, which dramatically affects how quartz colors appear. Colors with warm undertones glow beautifully in natural light. Cooler whites can appear almost blue in north-facing rooms. Always view samples in lighting conditions similar to your actual space.</p>

<h3>Step 3: Think About Scale</h3>

<p>Bold veining patterns like Brittanicca and Ironsbridge need enough surface area to breathe. On a small bathroom vanity, they can feel chaotic. On a large kitchen island, they become works of art. For smaller surfaces, consider more subtle patterns like Ella or Torquay.</p>

<h3>Step 4: Evaluate Your Maintenance Style</h3>

<p>While all Cambria colors are equally durable and maintenance-free, darker colors and matte finishes show fingerprints and water spots more readily than lighter polished surfaces. If low visual maintenance is a priority, lighter polished colors are more forgiving day to day.</p>

<h3>Step 5: Match Your Metal Finishes</h3>

<p>Hardware and fixtures play a significant role. Gold and brass fixtures pair naturally with warm-toned Cambria colors like Brittanicca Gold and Swanbridge. Chrome and nickel complement cooler whites like Ella and Torquay. Matte black hardware works with virtually any Cambria color.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions About Cambria Quartz Colors</h2>

<h3>How many colors does Cambria offer?</h3>
<p>Cambria offers over 200 designs across multiple collections, making it one of the largest color libraries in the quartz industry. New designs are introduced regularly, often inspired by trending natural stone patterns and evolving design preferences. Not all colors are available at every dealer, so visiting an authorized showroom like Griffin Quartz ensures you see the full range.</p>

<h3>What is the most popular Cambria quartz color?</h3>
<p>Brittanicca is consistently Cambria's best-selling color nationwide and here in South Florida. Its bold gray veining on a white background captures the look of Calacatta marble and works with virtually any kitchen design. Brittanicca Warm and Ella round out the top three.</p>

<h3>Does Cambria offer matte finish options?</h3>
<p>Yes. Cambria's proprietary Matte finish is available on select colors and creates a soft, honed look that you cannot get from other quartz brands. The matte finish reduces glare, hides fingerprints less effectively than polished but offers a distinctly luxurious tactile feel. Blackpool Matte is the most popular matte option.</p>

<h3>Can I use the same Cambria color in my kitchen and bathrooms?</h3>
<p>Absolutely, and many South Florida homeowners do exactly this for a cohesive flow throughout the home. Colors like Brittanicca Warm and Ella transition beautifully from kitchen countertops to master bathroom vanities to guest bath surfaces. Using the same color throughout can also reduce material costs through volume pricing.</p>

<h3>How does Cambria compare in price to other quartz brands?</h3>
<p>Cambria is a premium-priced brand, typically 15-30% more than mid-range options like Silestone or Caesarstone. However, the lifetime transferable warranty, American manufacturing, and NSF certification add substantial long-term value. For homeowners planning to stay in their home or seeking maximum resale value, the investment is well justified.</p>

<h3>Will my Cambria countertop look exactly like the sample?</h3>
<p>Cambria is engineered quartz, so it offers much greater consistency than natural stone. However, slight variations between production runs are normal and expected, particularly in designs with dramatic veining. We always recommend viewing your actual slab before fabrication to confirm you love the specific piece.</p>

<h3>Does Cambria work for outdoor kitchens in South Florida?</h3>
<p>Cambria, like all quartz countertops, is not recommended for outdoor installations exposed to direct sunlight. Prolonged UV exposure can cause the resin binders to yellow and degrade over time. For outdoor kitchens, we recommend granite or quartzite. However, Cambria works well in covered outdoor spaces like lanais and screened patios that are shielded from direct sun.</p>

<h3>How do I clean and maintain Cambria countertops?</h3>
<p>Cambria requires minimal maintenance. Clean daily with warm water and mild soap. For stubborn stains, a non-abrasive household cleaner works well. Cambria never needs sealing, polishing, or reconditioning. Avoid placing extremely hot pots directly on the surface, and use cutting boards to protect against knife scratches. In South Florida's humid climate, Cambria's non-porous surface resists moisture and mold growth naturally.</p>

<h2>What Is Quartz? A Quick Refresher</h2>

<p><strong>Quartz countertops</strong> are engineered stone surfaces made from approximately 90-94% ground natural quartz crystals bound together with polymer resins and pigments. They are manufactured in a factory under intense heat and pressure, resulting in a completely non-porous, consistent, and highly durable surface.</p>

<p>Because quartz is engineered, manufacturers can control the color, pattern, and veining with precision. This is what allows brands like Cambria, Silestone, and Caesarstone to produce marble-look quartz that closely mimics natural stone without any of the inherent vulnerabilities. Learn more in our <a href="/services/quartz-countertops">quartz countertops service page</a>.</p>

<h2>What Is Marble? A Quick Refresher</h2>

<p><strong>Marble</strong> is a natural metamorphic rock formed when limestone is subjected to extreme heat and pressure deep within the earth's crust. This geological process creates the distinctive veining, color variations, and translucent depth that make marble one of the most prized building materials in history.</p>

<p>Every marble slab is genuinely unique — no two pieces are identical. The most famous varieties include Calacatta (bold gold and gray veining), Carrara (softer gray veining), and Statuario (dramatic, high-contrast veining). Marble has graced palaces, cathedrals, and luxury homes for millennia. Explore our <a href="/services/marble-countertops">marble countertop options</a> for South Florida projects.</p>

<h2>Durability and Maintenance: A Clear Divide</h2>

<p>This is where the <strong>quartz vs marble</strong> debate has a definitive answer. Quartz wins on durability and maintenance by a wide margin.</p>

<h3>Quartz Durability</h3>

<p>Quartz rates 7 on the Mohs hardness scale, making it one of the hardest countertop materials available. It resists scratches from knives, impacts from dropped objects, and abrasion from daily use. Its non-porous surface means liquids cannot penetrate the material, so stains from wine, coffee, oil, and acidic foods wipe away effortlessly. Quartz never needs sealing, reconditioning, or special cleaners. Learn more in our <a href="/blog/how-to-clean-quartz-countertops">quartz cleaning guide</a>.</p>

<h3>Marble Durability</h3>

<p>Marble rates just 3 to 5 on the Mohs hardness scale, making it significantly softer than quartz. It scratches more easily, chips on exposed edges, and — most critically — <strong>etches from contact with acidic substances</strong>. Lemon juice, vinegar, tomato sauce, wine, and even certain cleaning products leave dull marks on marble surfaces. These etch marks are not stains; they are chemical reactions that dissolve the stone's surface.</p>

<p>Marble also requires sealing every 6 to 12 months to maintain its stain resistance. In South Florida's humid climate, where moisture exposure is constant, sealer breakdown can accelerate. Missed sealings leave the stone vulnerable to deep staining.</p>

<h3>The Maintenance Reality</h3>

<p>Owning marble countertops is a commitment. You will need to:</p>

<ul>
    <li>Seal the surface every 6-12 months (or hire a professional to do it)</li>
    <li>Immediately wipe up acidic spills before they etch the surface</li>
    <li>Use cutting boards religiously to avoid scratches</li>
    <li>Use coasters under glasses to prevent water rings</li>
    <li>Use pH-neutral cleaners specifically formulated for natural stone</li>
    <li>Accept that the surface will develop a patina over time</li>
</ul>

<p>Quartz requires none of this. Clean with soap and water. That is genuinely the entire maintenance routine.</p>

<h2>Aesthetic Comparison: Can Quartz Really Look Like Marble?</h2>

<p>This is the question that matters most to many homeowners. The short answer: <strong>yes, modern quartz can look remarkably like marble</strong>. The longer answer involves some nuance.</p>

<h3>Where Marble Still Wins</h3>

<p>Natural marble has a translucent depth that occurs because light penetrates slightly into the stone's surface before reflecting back. This creates a luminous, almost glowing quality that even the best engineered quartz has not fully replicated. The veining in marble is also genuinely random and organic — it formed over millions of years of geological activity, and no two slabs share the same pattern.</p>

<h3>Where Quartz Has Closed the Gap</h3>

<p>Premium marble-look quartz from brands like Cambria, Silestone, and Caesarstone has narrowed the aesthetic gap dramatically over the past several years. Modern production techniques create veining patterns that look convincingly natural, with depth and movement that were not possible a decade ago. Colors like <a href="/blog/calacatta-quartz-countertops">Calacatta-inspired quartz</a> are so realistic that many visitors cannot tell the difference at a glance.</p>

<p>The consistency of quartz is actually an advantage for large projects. With marble, adjacent slabs may look noticeably different, creating mismatches at seams. Quartz delivers slab-to-slab consistency that makes seam matching far easier, especially for large kitchen islands and L-shaped countertops.</p>

<h2>Best for Kitchens: Which Wins?</h2>

<p><strong>Quartz wins for kitchen countertops, and it is not particularly close.</strong></p>

<p>Kitchens are the highest-traffic, most demanding surfaces in any home. They endure acidic foods, hot pans, knife work, spilled liquids, and constant cleaning. Marble's softness, porosity, and vulnerability to etching make it a high-maintenance choice for a space that demands low-maintenance performance.</p>

<p>The exception is homeowners who view marble's patina as character rather than damage. Some people genuinely love the way marble ages and develops a lived-in quality over time. If that philosophy appeals to you, marble can be a beautiful kitchen choice — as long as you go in with realistic expectations about maintenance and aging.</p>

<p>For everyone else, marble-look quartz delivers the aesthetic without the anxiety. You get the Calacatta or Carrara look, the bold veining, and the luxurious feel — plus the freedom to set down a wine glass without panicking.</p>

<h2>Best for Bathrooms: A Closer Call</h2>

<p>The <strong>quartz vs marble bathroom</strong> decision is more nuanced than the kitchen debate because bathrooms present different challenges.</p>

<h3>Why Quartz Still Has the Edge</h3>

<p>Bathrooms expose countertops to constant moisture, soap residue, cosmetics, hair products, and cleaning chemicals. Quartz handles all of these without issue. Its non-porous surface never absorbs moisture, never develops mold, and never needs sealing — significant advantages in South Florida's humid environment.</p>

<h3>Where Marble Works in Bathrooms</h3>

<p>Bathrooms are generally lower-traffic than kitchens, with less exposure to acidic foods. A bathroom vanity will not encounter lemon juice and tomato sauce. The primary threats are cosmetics, soap, and moisture — less aggressive than kitchen hazards. Marble vanity tops in guest bathrooms, powder rooms, and primary bathrooms can be absolutely stunning and are more practical than marble in kitchens.</p>

<p>That said, certain hair products, perfumes, and cleaning agents are acidic enough to etch marble. The maintenance commitment remains.</p>

<h2>South Florida Climate Considerations</h2>

<p>South Florida's unique climate adds factors to the <strong>quartz vs marble</strong> decision that homeowners in other regions do not face.</p>

<h3>Humidity</h3>

<p>South Florida humidity regularly exceeds 70-80%. Marble is porous, meaning it absorbs ambient moisture from the air. Over time, this can lead to darkening, moisture spots, and accelerated sealer breakdown. Quartz is completely non-porous and unaffected by humidity — a meaningful advantage in our climate.</p>

<h3>Salt Air</h3>

<p>Coastal properties in Fort Lauderdale, Miami Beach, Boca Raton, and Palm Beach face salt-air exposure. Salt can affect marble sealers and accelerate surface deterioration. Quartz is inert and unaffected by salt exposure.</p>

<h3>Hurricane Preparedness</h3>

<p>Power outages during hurricane season can leave homes without air conditioning for extended periods. Without climate control, indoor humidity spikes dramatically. Marble surfaces can absorb this moisture and develop staining. Quartz remains completely unaffected regardless of temperature or humidity fluctuations.</p>

<h3>Outdoor Spaces</h3>

<p>Neither quartz nor marble is ideal for fully exposed outdoor installations. However, South Florida's many covered lanais and screened patios are common locations for wet bars and prep areas. In these covered spaces, quartz outperforms marble due to its superior moisture resistance.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions: Quartz vs Marble</h2>

<h3>Is quartz cheaper than marble?</h3>
<p>Entry-level quartz is generally less expensive than entry-level marble, and mid-range quartz is comparable to or slightly less than mid-range marble. However, the real cost difference emerges over time. Quartz has zero ongoing maintenance costs, while marble requires regular sealing ($200-$400/year), specialized cleaners, and occasional professional restoration. Over 15 years, quartz costs significantly less to own than marble.</p>

<h3>Can you tell the difference between quartz and marble?</h3>
<p>In person, experienced stone professionals can usually tell the difference, primarily because natural marble has a translucent depth and organic randomness to its veining that engineered quartz has not fully replicated. However, premium marble-look quartz from brands like Cambria is realistic enough that most casual observers cannot distinguish them. From across a room or in photographs, the difference is nearly indistinguishable.</p>

<h3>Does marble stain easily?</h3>
<p>Yes. Marble is porous and absorbs liquids if not properly sealed. Wine, coffee, oil, and colored sauces can all cause permanent staining on unsealed or poorly sealed marble. Even with proper sealing, marble etches from contact with acidic substances like lemon juice, vinegar, and certain cleaning products. Etching leaves dull, light-colored marks that are different from stains and cannot be removed with cleaning alone — they require professional honing.</p>

<h3>Is marble OK for bathroom vanities?</h3>
<p>Marble can work well for bathroom vanities, especially in guest bathrooms and powder rooms with lighter use. It is a more practical choice for bathrooms than for kitchens because bathrooms have less exposure to acidic foods. However, many cosmetics, hair products, and cleaning agents are acidic enough to etch marble. Quartz remains the lower-maintenance option for any bathroom.</p>

<h3>Why do people still choose marble over quartz?</h3>
<p>People choose marble for its unmatched natural beauty, translucent depth, and the prestige associated with genuine natural stone. Some homeowners appreciate marble's patina — the way it ages and develops character over time — as a feature rather than a flaw. Marble also has superior heat resistance, making it the preferred surface for serious bakers who roll pastry directly on the cool stone surface. For some, nothing but the real thing will do.</p>

<h3>Can quartz be used outside in South Florida?</h3>
<p>Quartz is not recommended for outdoor installations exposed to direct sunlight. UV exposure can cause the resin binders to yellow and degrade over time. For outdoor kitchens, granite or quartzite are better choices. Covered outdoor areas like screened lanais are generally acceptable for quartz, though you should verify the specific warranty terms with the manufacturer.</p>

<h3>Which adds more resale value: quartz or marble?</h3>
<p>Both add significant resale value, but in different ways. Marble signals luxury and prestige, potentially commanding a premium with buyers who appreciate natural stone. Quartz appeals to a broader range of buyers because of its low maintenance and lifetime warranty. In South Florida's competitive real estate market, quartz countertops from premium brands like Cambria are widely recognized as a high-value feature that appeals to both luxury and practical-minded buyers.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions About Commercial Countertops</h2>

<h3>What is the best countertop material for a restaurant bar?</h3>
<p>Quartz is the best overall material for restaurant bars. It is non-porous (so it will not stain from alcohol, cocktail acids, or food), NSF certified for food contact (select brands like Cambria), extremely durable against glass and dishware impacts, and requires no sealing or special maintenance. Dark quartz colors like Cambria Blackpool Matte are particularly popular because they hide the visual wear of nightly service. Granite is a secondary option offering superior heat resistance but requiring regular sealing.</p>

<h3>Do commercial countertops need to be ADA compliant?</h3>
<p>Yes, any countertop in a commercial space that serves the public must include ADA-accessible sections. At minimum, one section of a service counter must be no higher than 36 inches with appropriate knee clearance underneath. Specific requirements vary by application — hotel front desks, medical office reception windows, restaurant host stands, and retail checkout areas all have compliance obligations. We build ADA compliance into every commercial design.</p>

<h3>How long do commercial countertops last?</h3>
<p>Lifespan depends on the material and application. Premium quartz in a hotel vanity can last 20-25 years or more with normal use. A restaurant bar top in a high-volume establishment may last 12-20 years before showing significant wear. Marble in high-traffic areas may need professional restoration every 3-5 years and eventual replacement after 10-15 years. Solid surface typically lasts 10-15 years before requiring resurfacing or replacement.</p>

<h3>What is the minimum order size for commercial pricing?</h3>
<p>At Griffin Quartz, volume pricing begins at approximately 20 units or 500 square feet of material. The exact threshold varies by material and project complexity. Even smaller commercial projects receive competitive pricing based on the overall scope of work. We are happy to provide a detailed estimate for projects of any size.</p>

<h3>Can you match existing countertops in a renovation?</h3>
<p>With quartz, matching existing surfaces is generally achievable because quartz is manufactured to consistent color specifications. We can source the same color and production batch to ensure a close match. With natural stone, exact matching is nearly impossible because every slab is unique. For renovation projects involving natural stone, we recommend selecting a complementary rather than matching material unless sufficient original material was reserved.</p>

<p>Our primary service area covers South Florida from Jupiter to Miami, including all of Palm Beach, Broward, and Miami-Dade counties. Silestone offers a 25-year warranty for commercial applications. Caesarstone provides a lifetime residential warranty but a more limited commercial warranty. We help you understand the specific warranty terms for your chosen material and application during the specification process.</p>

<h2>Why Quartz Is Ideal for South Florida Bathrooms</h2>

<p>South Florida's climate creates specific challenges for bathroom surfaces that make quartz the clear winner for most homeowners.</p>

<h3>Humidity Resistance</h3>
<p>With average humidity levels of 70-80% and bathrooms generating additional steam from showers and baths, moisture is a constant presence. Quartz's completely non-porous surface is impervious to this moisture. Porous materials like marble and granite absorb humidity and can develop dark spots, mold growth, or surface haze over time.</p>

<h3>Mold and Mildew Prevention</h3>
<p>South Florida's warm, humid climate creates ideal conditions for mold and mildew. On porous natural stone surfaces, mold can take hold in microscopic pores, especially around the sink and faucet areas where water pools. Quartz eliminates this risk entirely.</p>

<h3>Low Maintenance in a Busy Lifestyle</h3>
<p>South Florida living emphasizes lifestyle and leisure, not household maintenance. Quartz's clean-with-soap-and-water simplicity fits the South Florida ethos perfectly. No annual sealing appointments. No specialty cleaners. No anxiety about cosmetic spills.</p>

<h3>Hurricane Season Readiness</h3>
<p>During extended power outages from hurricanes, homes can experience extreme humidity without air conditioning. Quartz surfaces are completely unaffected by these conditions. Natural stone surfaces, especially those overdue for resealing, can absorb moisture and develop staining during prolonged high-humidity exposure.</p>

    <li><strong>Eased edge:</strong> Flat top with slightly rounded corners. The most popular choice for modern and transitional bathrooms. Clean, simple, and timeless.</li>
    <li><strong>Beveled edge:</strong> Angled cut along the top edge that adds visual dimension. Popular in transitional and traditional bathrooms.</li>
    <li><strong>Bullnose (half or full):</strong> Rounded edge that provides maximum safety and a soft, classic look. Ideal for family bathrooms and children's bathrooms.</li>
    <li><strong>Ogee edge:</strong> S-shaped decorative profile that adds elegance to traditional and luxury bathrooms. Popular on marble and marble-look quartz vanities.</li>
    <li><strong>Mitered edge:</strong> Creates the appearance of a thicker slab by joining a vertical piece to the edge. Dramatic and modern, especially effective with bold veining patterns.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Edge Profile and Countertop Thickness</h3>

<p>Bathroom vanity countertops are typically fabricated in either 2cm (0.75 inch) or 3cm (1.25 inch) thickness. The choice affects edge profile options and overall appearance. For a detailed guide on thickness considerations, see our <a href="/blog/countertop-thickness-guide">countertop thickness guide</a>.</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>2cm (0.75 inch):</strong> Lighter weight, lower material cost, requires support from the vanity base. Standard for most bathroom vanities.</li>
    <li><strong>3cm (1.25 inch):</strong> More substantial look, self-supporting on most vanity bases, allows for more decorative edge profiles. Popular in luxury bathroom designs.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Installation Considerations for Bathroom Vanity Countertops</h2>

<p>Bathroom countertop installation requires attention to several factors that differ from kitchen installations.</p>

<h3>Plumbing Coordination</h3>
<p>Sink cutout placement must align precisely with existing or planned plumbing. Our digital templating process captures exact plumbing locations to ensure a perfect fit. If you are moving plumbing as part of a renovation, complete the plumbing rough-in before templating.</p>

<h3>Backsplash Options</h3>
<p>Bathroom vanities typically include either a 4-inch stone backsplash (matching the countertop) or a full-height tile backsplash. A stone backsplash provides a clean, coordinated look and protects the wall from water splashes around the sink and faucet areas.</p>

<h3>Faucet Holes</h3>
<p>Faucet hole placement depends on whether you choose a deck-mounted or wall-mounted faucet. Deck-mounted faucets require precise hole drilling in the countertop — single-hole, widespread (8-inch centers), or centerset (4-inch centers). Wall-mounted faucets require no countertop holes, creating a cleaner surface but requiring wall plumbing modifications.</p>

<h3>Vanity Cabinet Condition</h3>
<p>The vanity cabinet must be level, structurally sound, and properly secured to the wall before countertop installation. For 2cm countertops, the cabinet provides essential support. For 3cm countertops, the material is heavier and the cabinet must be rated for the additional weight.</p>

<h3>Access Considerations</h3>
<p>Bathroom installations can be more challenging than kitchen work due to tighter spaces, narrower doorways, and limited maneuvering room. Our installation team plans access routes in advance to ensure safe, damage-free delivery and placement.</p>

<h3>Timing in a Renovation Sequence</h3>
<p>In a bathroom renovation, countertop installation typically occurs after vanity cabinet installation and before final plumbing connections (sink and faucet installation). Tile work on walls should ideally be completed before the countertop is templated to ensure accurate measurements against the finished wall surface.</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions About Bathroom Vanity Countertops</h2>

<h3>What is the best material for bathroom vanity countertops?</h3>
<p>Quartz is the best overall material for bathroom vanity countertops. It is completely non-porous, meaning it will not absorb water, soap, or cosmetics. It never needs sealing, resists mold and mildew growth, and handles bathroom cleaning chemicals without staining or etching. In South Florida's humid climate, quartz's moisture immunity is a particularly significant advantage. Marble is a beautiful alternative for low-traffic guest baths, but it requires ongoing sealing and maintenance.</p>

<h3>How much does a bathroom vanity countertop cost?</h3>
<p>In South Florida, a quartz bathroom vanity countertop typically costs $350-$1,800 installed, depending on the vanity size, material grade, and design details. A standard 36-inch single vanity in mid-range quartz runs approximately $450-$900 installed. A 60-inch double vanity in premium quartz ranges from $800-$1,500 installed. These prices include material, fabrication, sink cutout, standard edge, and installation.</p>

<h3>Should I get a 2cm or 3cm bathroom vanity top?</h3>
<p>Both work well for bathrooms. A 2cm (0.75 inch) top is the traditional standard for bathroom vanities — it is lighter, less expensive, and perfectly functional for a vanity application. A 3cm (1.25 inch) top provides a more substantial, luxurious look and matches the thickness of most kitchen countertops. If you are using the same quartz color in your kitchen and bathroom, matching the 3cm thickness creates a cohesive feel throughout the home.</p>

<h3>What sink type is best for a quartz vanity?</h3>
<p>Undermount sinks are the most popular and practical choice for quartz vanity countertops. They create a clean, seamless look and make it easy to wipe water and debris directly into the sink. Vessel sinks offer a more dramatic aesthetic but sit higher and create more splash potential. Integrated sinks provide the ultimate in seamless hygiene but cost more and limit your sink shape options.</p>

<h3>Can I use quartz for a shower bench or tub surround?</h3>
<p>Yes. Quartz works well for shower benches, tub decks, and even shower niches. Its non-porous surface handles constant water exposure better than natural stone alternatives. Many South Florida homeowners coordinate their vanity countertop, shower bench, and tub deck in the same quartz color for a cohesive bathroom design.</p>

<h3>How long does a bathroom vanity countertop last?</h3>
<p>A quality quartz vanity countertop can last 25 years or more with normal use. Premium brands like Cambria back their products with lifetime warranties that cover residential use. Marble vanity tops can also last decades but require ongoing maintenance to maintain their appearance. Cultured marble and solid surface typically last 10-15 years before showing significant wear.</p>

<h3>Do I need a backsplash on my bathroom vanity?</h3>
<p>While not structurally required, a backsplash is strongly recommended for bathroom vanities. The area behind the faucet is exposed to constant water splashing, and a backsplash protects the wall from water damage, staining, and mold growth. A 4-inch stone backsplash matching your countertop is the most common choice and is typically the most cost-effective option. Full-height tile backsplashes are an alternative that can add more visual impact.</p>

<h3>Can Griffin Quartz replace just the vanity top without replacing the cabinet?</h3>
<p>Yes. We regularly fabricate and install new vanity countertops on existing cabinets. As long as the cabinet is level, structurally sound, and properly secured, a new countertop can be templated, fabricated, and installed without disturbing the existing vanity base. This is one of the most cost-effective bathroom upgrades available — it dramatically transforms the room's appearance at a fraction of the cost of a full renovation.</p>
